The Power of Urgency#

We’ve all been there. Standing at the edge of our comfort zone, heart pounding, your dreams so close. Maybe it’s that book you’ve always talked about writing, or the marathon you’ve been putting off, or that ticket to a far-off place with a name you can barely pronounce.

You can see it, feel it, almost taste it. But when it’s time to turn that vision into reality, something strange happens. We freeze. And our dreams? They stay just that - dreams.

It’s like there’s an invisible wall holding us back. Our minds start racing with excuses: “I’m too tired”, “I’m not ready”, “What if I fail?”

That energy that was pushing us forward? It fades away, leaving us stuck on the wrong side of action.

This invisible barrier is tricky. It doesn’t show up with flashing lights or loud alarms. Instead, it whispers doubts, fills our heads with reasons to wait, to hold back, to stay cozy in our comfort zone.

But here’s the thing: this barrier, as solid as it feels, is actually as fragile as a soap bubble. All it takes is one bold move to pop it. Yet for many of us, that single step feels as hard as moving a mountain.

So how do we break through? How do we cross the gap between our big dreams and the actions we need to take?

The answer is in building something powerful enough to smash any barrier: a sense of urgency. As Elinor Smith once said:

“Successful people rarely sit back and let things happen. They go out and make things happen.”

The Burning Ships Strategy: A Lesson from History#

It’s the 3rd century BC, and the Persian coast is alive with activity. Many ships from Macedonia have just landed, their bottoms scratching the sand as they reach shore. Leading this attack is Alexander the Great, looking out for signs of the enemy.

But something is amiss. As reports filter in, a hard truth becomes clear: the Persian army outnumbers the Macedonians five to one. The odds are staggering. Winning battles is hard enough, but winning against an army five times your size? It seems impossible.

In the Macedonian camp, soldiers are worried. They look at each other with fear, speaking quietly about their small chance of winning. Military leaders throughout history know a simple truth: when morale is low, defeat is almost certain.

But Alexander is not just any leader. In this moment of crisis, he conceives a strategy so audacious it will echo through the ages.

“Burn all our ships,” he commands, his voice steady and resolute.

His officers stare in disbelief. Surely they’ve misheard. But no - Alexander repeats the order, his eyes shining with determination.

As flames engulf their means of retreat, the soldiers’ minds race. They had been contemplating their dire situation, wondering if it might be wiser to turn back, to live and fight another day. Now, that option has literally gone up in smoke.

In this moment, Alexander channels the wisdom of Sun Tzu, the legendary Chinese philosopher who would write in “The Art of War”:

“Bring the troops to a point where they have no way out, so that they must die before they can escape. If they are in grave danger, they will lose their fear. If they have nowhere to go, they will stand firm. If they have no choice, they will fight.”

The message of the burning ships is clear and terrifying at the same time: there is no going back. It’s victory or death.

This drastic action ignites something in the Macedonian army. The sense of urgency is palpable, electric. It courses through the ranks, transforming fear and doubt into fierce determination. With retreat no longer an option, they turn to face the enemy, their resolve hardened by the knowledge that everything hinges on this moment.

As they charge into battle, the impossible becomes possible. Against all odds, the Macedonians emerge victorious.

But Alexander’s triumph that day goes beyond the battlefield. By creating a situation of extreme urgency, he unlocks the full potential of his army. He proves a timeless truth: comfort breeds complacency, but urgency breeds greatness.

In the fires of those burning ships, Alexander forges more than just a military victory. He creates a lesson that will resonate through the centuries - a testament to the power of urgency in overcoming seemingly insurmountable odds.

Steve Jobs and the Power of Urgency: Echoes of Alexander’s Strategy#

The clock ticks relentlessly. One week until the launch of the first Macintosh. The air in the room is thick with tension as the engineering team approaches Steve Jobs, their faces etched with worry.

“It’s impossible we can make it in one week,” they plead, voices tinged with exhaustion. “We need one more week.”

Jobs stands before them, silent for a moment. His eyes scan the room, taking in the faces of his team. Then, channeling the spirit of Alexander the Great, he delivers his verdict:

“If you can do it in two weeks, you can do it in one”, Steve says.

The room falls silent. Engineers exchange worried glances, the weight of Jobs’ words settling on their shoulders. They know what this means - sleepless nights, skipped meals, a mad sprint to the finish line.

But Jobs isn’t finished. He fixes each team member with a piercing stare, his voice low and intense. “We’re not just launching a computer,” he says. “We’re starting a revolution. And revolutions wait for no one.”

In that moment, Jobs has done more than refuse a delay. He has created a crucible, a pressure cooker that will either forge his team into innovators capable of changing the world - or break them entirely.

Jobs understood a fundamental truth: to make a revolution in personal computers, intelligence alone is not enough. You need:

  • Resilience.
  • Grit.
  • A willingness to work incredibly hard.
  • A team brave and determined enough to challenge the status quo.

This approach wasn’t born from cruelty or unreasonableness. Jobs believed that many of our first impressions are often based on fear - fear of failure, fear of not meeting expectations, fear of innovating and falling short. And fear, he knew, is the great enemy of action and change.

In pushing his team to the limit, Jobs wasn’t just trying to meet a deadline. He was unlocking his team’s full potential, forcing them to confront their fears and overcome them. He understood that true innovation doesn’t happen in comfort zones - it happens when your back is against the wall and there’s no choice but to push forward.

A week later, the unthinkable happened. The engineers, driven by the sense of urgency Jobs had instilled in them, met the original deadline. The presentation was a success, marking the beginning of a new era in personal computing.

This story illustrates a powerful lesson: sometimes, the limitations we perceive are just that - perceptions. When pushed, when inspired, when filled with a sense of urgency and purpose, we are capable of far more than we imagine.

In the end, Jobs’ strategy wasn’t about bullying or unreasonable demands. It was about creating an environment where innovation could thrive, where ordinary people could achieve extraordinary things. It was about understanding that revolutions - whether in technology, business, or society - are built on the foundation of urgency, determination, and the courage to push beyond perceived limits.

The Urgency Principle: Elon Musk’s Relentless Drive#

In the high-stakes world of aerospace innovation, a tense scene unfolded between Elon Musk and Tom Mueller, head of SpaceX’s Merlin engine team. Mueller had just presented an aggressive timeline for completing a prototype, but Musk, ever the visionary, was far from satisfied.

“Cut it in half,” Musk demanded, his eyes locked on Mueller.

Incredulous, Mueller protested. “Such an aggressive schedule can’t be reduced any further,” he argued, his voice tinged with disbelief.

But Musk, unwavering in his conviction, refused to back down. “A manic sense of urgency is our operating principle,” he declared. It was clear that in Musk’s view, they weren’t acting with enough of it.

The ultimatum came swiftly and decisively. Musk stared Mueller in the face and delivered his final word: if Mueller wanted to keep his job as head of the engine team, he had to find a way to shorten the timeline. Faced with this stark choice, Mueller agreed.

What followed was a period of intense work and relentless effort. The team pushed themselves to the limit, striving to meet Musk’s seemingly impossible deadline. As the dust settled and the engine prototype was completed, a surprising truth emerged: Mueller had been right. The schedule Musk had imposed was indeed too aggressive.

But this raises a crucial question: Does this mean that a sense of urgency is unhelpful? Far from it.

The secret to SpaceX’s ability to revolutionize the aerospace industry lies in a powerful combination: first principles thinking coupled with an insane sense of urgency. This approach, while challenging, has made all the difference in the long run.

Mueller’s later reflection paints a vivid picture of this paradox:

“Even though we didn’t meet most of the schedules and cost targets that Elon had set, we outperformed all of our competitors. We were developing the most amazing rockets at the lowest cost in history, and we felt great about it in the end, even if Elon wasn’t happy with us.”

The Ultimate Urgency: Dostoyevsky’s Awakening#

St. Petersburg, 1849. A cold December morning. Fyodor Dostoyevsky, shackled and disoriented, is led into a square teeming with soldiers and onlookers. The air is thick with tension and the bitter Russian winter.

One year before, Dostoyevsky was arrested and imprisoned for the supposed involvement in a conspiracy against the russian tsar. The punishment: jail for a given period of time. But, one day, the tsar changed his plans.

On that square, Dostoyevsky suddenly noticed something. A scaffold, a priest, and coffins. His coffin. Time seems to stop as the reality sinks in: he’s about to die.

In that moment, facing his own mortality, Dostoyevsky’s life flashes before his eyes. Not in a blur of memories, but in stark clarity of wasted opportunities and unfulfilled potential.

But fate had other plans. Just as the executioner raises his hand, a messenger arrives, breathless. The Tsar has commuted the sentence - life instead of death, Siberian labor instead of the noose.

In the echoing silence of his reprieve, Dostoyevsky has an epiphany. He writes to his brother:

“When I look back on the past and think how much time I wasted in vain, how much of it was lost in illusions, in mistakes, in idleness, in the inability to live, how I did not value it, […] then my heart shrinks with sorrow. Life is a gift, life is happiness, every moment could have been an eternity of happiness”

As Confucius wisely noted:

“We have two lives: the second begins when we realize we have only one.”

Your Turn: Embracing Urgency in Everyday Life#

Here’s the thing: you don’t need to face execution or launch a revolutionary product to tap into the power of urgency. The secret? Create your own sense of urgency. Light your own fire.

Remember: inaction has a cost. So, let’s start small:

  1. Pick one goal or project you’ve been putting off
  2. Set an unrealistic deadline for it. Yes, unrealistic
  3. Tell someone about your deadline. Make yourself accountable
  4. Attack it with a manic sense of urgency
  5. Watch what happens

Will you hit your ambitious target? Maybe not. But you’ll likely achieve more than you would have otherwise. This little experiment can help you shatter those self-imposed limits and uncover reserves of energy and creativity you never knew you had.

Living with urgency means truly grasping that your time is limited. We all know this in our heads, but how often do we live it? If you carry the idea that one day it will all end, you’ll likely squeeze more life out of every moment.

In our fast-paced world, the most urgent thing we can do is stop waiting and start becoming. Your dreams, your goals, your life - they’re all waiting for you to take that leap.

Don’t wait for:

  • Someone to push you with extreme deadlines
  • A near-death experience to start living
  • The perfect moment

Embrace urgency today.

After all, the clock is ticking for all of us - how will you make your moments count?

The choice, and the urgency, is yours.
