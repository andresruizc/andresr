Take a step back

January 1, 2024 · 6 min
Table of Contents
  • The Water Epiphany
  • Sam Altman and His Gap Year
  • Conclusion: Take a Step Back to Move Forward

The lake was still, its surface a mirror reflecting the cloudy sky above. A young man sat in a boat, his muscles tense with frustration. This was Bruce Lee, the martial arts prodigy known as “Never Sit Still,” and he was doing something that felt utterly foreign to him: nothing.

But this moment of forced inaction would lead to an epiphany that would change the course of his life and redefine martial arts forever.

Bruce Lee’s journey began at age thirteen when he discovered his true passion: martial arts. From that moment on, he threw himself into training with a fervor that both impressed and worried his teacher, Yip Man. Day and night, Bruce practiced his moves, honing his body into a weapon of lightning-fast reflexes and devastating power. His dedication was unquestionable, his potential limitless.

Yet, something was missing.

Yip Man, a master of Wing Chun kung fu, saw beyond the physical prowess of his star pupil. He recognized that true mastery lay not just in strength and speed, but in understanding the deeper principles of martial arts.

“Never impose yourself on nature. It flows,” he would say. “Never oppose any problem head-on, but counter it by balancing with it.”

These philosophical nuggets fell on deaf ears. Young Bruce, consumed by his desire to perfect his techniques, dismissed them as irrelevant. There was no time for abstract ideas when there were punches to throw and kicks to perfect. He had adopted an all-or-nothing mentality, believing that any moment not spent in direct training was a moment wasted.

The Water Epiphany#

Yip Man knew he had to act. In a move that seemed counterintuitive, he banned Bruce from training for an entire week. Instead, he assigned his pupil a single task: reflect on the abstract teachings he had been ignoring.

For Bruce, this felt like torture. How could he possibly improve by doing nothing? His obsession with constant forward motion had blinded him to the value of stepping back. Restless and frustrated, he took to the water, hoping that the gentle lapping of waves against his boat might calm his racing mind.

As he lay back, pondering his master’s words, frustration boiled over. In a burst of anger, he lashed out at the water, unleashing a flurry of punches against the unyielding surface. And then… stillness. Both in the water and in Bruce’s mind.

In that moment of clarity, Bruce understood. The water, soft and yielding, had effortlessly absorbed his most powerful strikes. It didn’t fight back, didn’t resist. It simply was. By stepping back from his relentless training and allowing himself this moment of reflection, Bruce had taken two steps forward in his understanding of martial arts and life itself.

“I struck it with all my might,” Bruce would later write, “but it was not hurt. This water, the softest substance in the world, which could be contained in the smallest bottle, only seemed weak. In reality, it could penetrate the hardest substances in the world.”

This realization became the cornerstone of Bruce Lee’s philosophy, both in martial arts and in life.

“Be water, my friend,” he would famously say. “Empty your mind, be formless. Shapeless, like water. If you put water into a cup, it becomes the cup. You put water into a bottle and it becomes the bottle. You put it in a teapot, it becomes the teapot. Now, water can flow or it can crash. Be water, my friend.”

Sam Altman and His Gap Year#

Fast forward to some decades later, and we see an example of how taking a step back can really make a difference. At 26, fresh from selling his startup, Sam Altman made a decision that raised eyebrows in Silicon Valley: he took a year off. In a culture where your job defines your social status, where networking never stops, and where the fear of falling behind is palpable, Altman’s choice was bold and, to some, bewildering.

“If you are in the position between jobs where you can take a year off, I highly recommend it,” Altman reflects. “I think it was like one of the two or three best career things that I ever did.”

But it wasn’t easy. Altman vividly describes the social discomfort:

“It’s really hard to do in Silicon Valley because like in a place where social status is determined by your job and what you’re working on… when you show up at a party and someone says ‘Oh, what are you working on?’ and you’re like ‘Uh, I’m kind of just taking the year off’… you can sort of see in real time their eyes like look for someone else in the room to talk to. Doesn’t feel that good.”

Despite the social awkwardness, Altman used this time to expand his horizons in ways that would prove invaluable:

  • He read “many dozens of textbooks”
  • Learned about nuclear engineering
  • Delved into the emerging field of AI
  • Explored synthetic biology
  • Studied investing

“I didn’t have any idea that they were all going to come together in the way they did,” Altman admits. “Almost all of it didn’t work out, but the seeds were planted for things that worked in deep ways later.”

Conclusion: Take a Step Back to Move Forward#

In our hyper-connected, always-on world, the stories of Altman and Lee serve as powerful reminders of the value of pause. They challenge us to reconsider our notions of productivity and progress. Sometimes, the most productive thing we can do is to step back, reflect, and allow our minds to wander and wonder.

And it’s not just Bruce Lee or Sam Altman. Consider some the great minds throughout history who understood the principle of taking a step back:

  • Beethoven carried his musical scores on long walks, finding inspiration in the rhythm of his footsteps
  • Einstein played the violin when stuck on complex physics problems, allowing his subconscious to work its magic
  • Nietzsche boldly claimed, “Only ideas gained by walking are of any value”
  • Quentin Tarantino floats in his pool after a day of screenwriting, letting new ideas bubble to the surface

So the next time you feel the relentless pressure to keep pushing forward, remember Sam Altman at a Silicon Valley party, uncomfortable but committed to his year of exploration. Remember Bruce Lee, frustrated but on the verge of a breakthrough.

Consider giving yourself permission to pause, to reflect, to learn widely and wildly. After all, in that space of apparent inactivity, you might just find the idea that changes everything.

Be water, my friend. And remember, sometimes the most powerful move is to take a step back, allowing you to leap two steps forward.
